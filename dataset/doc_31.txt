Full Content:
In his inaugural address on Jan. 20 (local time), U.S. President Donald Trump declared his intention to dismantle the eco-friendly policies of the Biden administration, known as the Green New Deal. He emphasized, "We will end the Green New Deal and we will revoke the electric vehicle mandate, saving our auto industry" and allowing American citizens to "buy the car of your choice."
The immediate market reaction to President Trump's statements was stark. Investor sentiment took a sharp downturn as the new administration's stance on electric vehicles became clear. The stock prices of domestic secondary battery companies, which are integral to the electric vehicle market, plummeted from the first day of Trump's presidency.
EcoPro BM closed at 126,100 won, down 11,900 won (-8.62%) from the previous trading day. At one point during the day, EcoPro BM's stock price had fallen more than 10%. This sharp decline is in stark contrast to the 6% surge the previous day, which was driven by low-priced buying.
The broader market for secondary battery-related companies also experienced significant declines. Kosdaq-listed companies EcoPro (-5.87%) and Enchem (-8.39%), as well as the leading domestic secondary battery company LG Energy Solution (-4.32%), could not avoid the sharp downturn.
President Trump's remarks were interpreted by the market as a signal to withdraw subsidies and tax credits for electric vehicle purchases, which had been promoted by the Biden administration. This policy shift is expected to have far-reaching implications for the electric vehicle market and the secondary battery industry, both of which have been heavily reliant on government incentives to drive growth.
Donald Trump's previous term as President was marked by a deregulatory agenda, including rolling back environmental regulations and withdrawing from the Paris Climate Agreement. His stance on eco-friendly initiatives has been consistent with his earlier policies.
The Green New Deal, supported by the Biden administration, aimed to address climate change and economic inequality through measures such as transitioning to renewable energy sources and reducing greenhouse gas emissions. The electric vehicle market, which has been growing rapidly, benefited from government incentives like tax credits and subsidies. The secondary battery industry, producing essential components for electric vehicles, has seen significant growth tied to these policies.
The potential withdrawal of support for electric vehicles not only affects the secondary battery industry but also has broader implications for the automotive manufacturing and renewable energy sectors.
