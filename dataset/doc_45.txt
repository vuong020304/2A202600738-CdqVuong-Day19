          Airline Marketing: When Technology Goes Too Far

I read an article yesterday with the headline, "Airlines Looking to Use Personalized Marketing in Seatbacks and In-Flight Apps." My first thought was, "Holy crap, how about you worry about taking the plane off on time and landing it with all its pieces still attached?"

The Perception Problem

Right now, we can all admit the airline industry's perception is pretty bad. Whether it’s true or not, every time you scroll through social media, you see stories about airline fails. After reading the article, I took away two things that are very relevant for business owners.

Perception Matters

The first takeaway is that perception matters. The perception of your business or industry directly affects your advertising costs and effectiveness. This is something many people overlook. You need to consider how your business is perceived while planning your marketing and advertising strategies. If people have a negative view of your industry, your ads will have to work much harder.

The Creep Factor

The second takeaway is about personalized ads on seatback entertainment systems. Personally, I find this a bit creepy. Just because we have the technology to do something doesn’t mean we should. When planning your marketing, if your first thought is "this is kind of creepy," it’s probably best to rethink it.

Lessons for Business Owners

Here’s what business owners can learn from this:
Monitor Your Perception: Regularly check how your business and industry are perceived by the public. Adjust your marketing strategies accordingly to address any negative perceptions.

Avoid the Creep Factor: Use technology wisely. If a marketing tactic feels invasive or creepy, it can turn customers away. Stick to strategies that respect your audience’s privacy and comfort.

Final Thoughts

The airline industry’s push for personalized marketing on in-flight apps and seatbacks raises important questions about how we use technology in marketing. Perception and ethical considerations are crucial. Just because you can do something doesn’t mean you should. Always keep your audience’s perception and comfort in mind.

Key Findings: Summer and Fall Travel Planning are “Full Speed Ahead!”

Is the American traveler’s interest in using EV’s on their trips losing a bit of steam? Only 24% indicated using an electric vehicle in their travels over the past six months, down 5 points since August of 2023. In addition, only 26% say that they are either likely or somewhat likely to use an electric vehicle (EV) on their next trip, down from 37% just over a year ago (April 2023). Could it have something to do with the decline in overall EV sales and/or the lack of charging station infrastructure for longer and remote road trip travel?  Or is it something else?  Longwoods and Miles Partnership will be taking a deeper look at EV travel later this summer!
